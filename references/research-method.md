# Research Method

Use this method for research-led and blended weekend guides. The objective is a
body of evidence strong enough to support a clear reading experience, not a
large pile of links.

## 1. Turn the reader promise into research questions

Break the governing question into five to eight answerable questions. Include
the forms that matter for the topic:

- definitions and boundaries;
- history and causal development;
- mechanisms and incentives;
- scale, prevalence, or measurable effects;
- important cases and first-hand experience;
- competing interpretations and counterexamples;
- practical consequences;
- current uncertainties and likely developments.

Mark which questions are essential to the reader promise and which are optional.

## 2. Make a source plan

Choose sources according to the claim rather than using one hierarchy for
everything:

- official records, legislation, standards, datasets, filings, and archival
  material for primary facts;
- peer-reviewed research and serious scholarly books for established knowledge
  and contested findings;
- first-hand interviews, speeches, memoirs, field reports, and practitioner
  writing for lived experience and operating detail;
- high-quality reporting for events, investigation, and synthesis;
- credible industry analysis for market structure and practice, with attention
  to incentives and methodology.

Use aggregators and search snippets to discover sources, not as evidence when
the original is available. For time-sensitive claims, record the publication
date and the date the underlying event or data refers to.

## 3. Research in widening circles

### Orientation pass

Learn the vocabulary, major disputes, canonical sources, and boundaries of the
topic. Use this pass to improve the questions, not to collect decorative facts.

### Evidence pass

Open and inspect the best sources for each essential question. Capture the
specific passage, table, finding, or observation needed. Follow citations back
to originals when practical.

### Challenge pass

Search deliberately for contrary evidence, failed cases, methodological
criticism, geographic or demographic limits, and views outside the dominant
account. A serious guide should reveal where its preferred explanation bends.

### Gap pass

Compare the evidence with the outline. Research only the gaps that prevent a
chapter from performing its contract. Stop when additional sources repeat what
is already well established without changing the piece.

## 4. Keep a source ledger

For each source, record:

- full title, author or institution, publication, date, and URL or stable
  identifier;
- source type and why it is credible for this claim;
- the exact claim, number, quotation, or scene detail it supports;
- relevant page, section, table, timestamp, or passage location;
- limitations, conflicts of interest, population, geography, and time period;
- which chapter may use it.

Keep quotations short in the ledger unless analysis requires more context.
Never copy a source's phrasing into the draft without marking it as a quotation.

## 5. Build an evidence map

Before drafting, map each chapter contract to its key claims and sources. Use a
compact table or list with these fields:

| Chapter | Claim or question | Evidence | Source | Confidence or caveat |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |

Flag unsupported load-bearing claims. Either research them, reframe them as
reasoned interpretation, soften them, or remove them.

## 6. Use sources in prose

- Lead with the idea or observation, not a parade of citations.
- Attribute a source when its identity helps the reader assess the claim.
- Explain what a number measures before telling the reader what it means.
- Place quotations where the speaker's actual language adds something that a
  paraphrase cannot.
- Separate reported fact from the guide's interpretation.
- State the limits of evidence near the claim when those limits affect its
  meaning.
- Avoid stacking several weak sources to simulate one strong source.

For inline-link Markdown, link the most specific supporting page close to the
claim. For endnotes, number notes consistently and retain enough bibliographic
detail for the reader to find the source.

## 7. Verify before delivery

Reopen sources for every load-bearing number, quotation, and surprising claim.
Check whether later corrections or newer data change the account. Confirm that
the draft does not widen a study's population, convert correlation into cause,
or present an estimate as a count.

If a source cannot be recovered or a claim cannot be verified, cut or qualify
the claim. Never fill the gap with a plausible citation.
