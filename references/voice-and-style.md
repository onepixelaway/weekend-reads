# Voice and Style

Use this reference to make a long guide composed, lucid, and enjoyable across a
weekend sitting. The desired voice is documentary with a touch of explainer: it
observes carefully, names things plainly, and does not perform intelligence for
the reader.

## The register

- **Composed and curious.** Convey confidence without hype or omniscience.
- **An invisible narrator.** Avoid default “I.” Use a declared first-person
  perspective only when the author's experience is part of the evidence.
- **A respected reader.** Assume intelligence and patience, not prior expertise.
  Use “you” sparingly and purposefully.
- **Concrete before abstract.** Give the reader something visible or graspable,
  then explain the larger idea.
- **Unhurried but economical.** Depth comes from useful detail and connection,
  not from saying the same thing three ways.

Do not imitate a named living writer. Translate stylistic requests into general
traits such as precision, warmth, narrative distance, or sentence density.

## Write a calibration paragraph

Before drafting the body, write one paragraph on the real subject in the target
register. It is a tuning fork, not necessarily the opening. Compare later prose
against it and rewrite passages that drift toward a blog post, self-help book,
academic paper, sales deck, or generic AI answer.

Reference example for the feel only; do not reuse its wording:

> Before databases, there were ledgers. For centuries, merchants returned to
> ruled books ordered by account, using them to decide what to buy, what to
> sell, and whom to trust. The ledger was not a diary and not quite an archive.
> It was a working record, built to carry decisions across time. Its forms have
> changed repeatedly. Its purpose has proved more durable.

## Sustain a long read

### Create movement

Move between scales: a person and a system, an object and its history, a claim
and its evidence, a mechanism and its consequence. Do not remain at the level
of summary for pages at a time.

Use unresolved tensions as quiet propulsion. The reader should continue because
the picture is becoming richer, not because the prose withholds a trick answer.

### Add texture honestly

Use concrete cases, artifacts, places, processes, and first-hand voices when
they clarify the subject. Verify every scene-like detail. If a source does not
establish the weather, expression, room, sequence, or quotation, do not supply
it from imagination.

### Give the reader places to rest

Use descriptive subheads, occasional short paragraphs, and moments of synthesis.
A long section may briefly gather what has changed before it continues. Avoid
administrative announcements such as “in this chapter we will explore.”

### Vary rhythm without showing off

Mix sentence and paragraph lengths according to the thought. Let a short
sentence carry a real turn. Avoid using fragments, one-line paragraphs, or
parallel clauses as a repeated dramatic device.

## Prefer familiar, exact language

Use the most familiar word that keeps the meaning. Do not choose a formal,
academic, technical, or business word merely because it sounds more precise.
If a reader could understand the same idea faster with an everyday word or a
short plain phrase, use it.

Choose the replacement from the sentence's actual meaning rather than making a
blind word swap. For example:

- use “use” instead of “utilize”;
- replace “lever” with “tool,” “advantage,” “control,” or “way to change it,”
  depending on what the sentence means;
- use “written version” or “turning speech into text” instead of
  “transcription” when the name of the technical process does not matter;
- prefer “the people involved” to “stakeholders,” “where things stand” to
  “shared state,” and “how it moves” to “dynamics” when those phrases are
  accurate.

Keep a specialized term when it carries meaning that a simpler phrase would
lose. Explain it in ordinary language on first use, preferably in the same
sentence, then use it consistently. Simple language must not make the idea
vague, childish, or wrong.

During revision, read for vocabulary separately from rhythm. Ask of each
sentence: could a curious non-specialist understand this on the first pass
without stopping over a word? Rewrite when the answer is no and the difficult
word is not necessary.

Identify a person, institution, place, or specialized tool on first mention.
Give only the context the reader needs, then use the short name.

## Remove common machine-prose habits

Scan for these tendencies and rewrite them:

- em dashes used as the default hinge in sentence after sentence;
- “it is not X, it is Y” antitheses that create false drama;
- lists padded to exactly three items for rhythm;
- repeated “from X to Y” constructions;
- throat-clearing such as “it is worth noting,” “in today's world,” or “at the
  end of the day”;
- filler intensifiers such as “truly,” “incredibly,” “really,” “simply,” and
  “genuinely”;
- hollow importance claims such as “this is crucial” without showing why;
- mechanical previews and recaps at every chapter boundary;
- neat, uplifting conclusions that erase unresolved difficulty.

Do not turn this list into a visible gimmick. Natural prose may occasionally use
one of these forms. Repetition and reflex are the problem.

## Handle evidence gracefully

- Attribute specific claims close enough that the reader can tell what the
  source supports.
- Prefer original sources. Use a secondary source when its synthesis or
  reporting is itself valuable.
- Introduce quotations with enough context to make the speaker and occasion
  meaningful.
- Quote only the words that matter. Paraphrase the rest accurately.
- Give strong counterarguments their best form.
- Distinguish observation, reported fact, estimate, interpretation, and
  projection.
- Never let citations create a false impression of certainty.

## Mechanics

- Use content-bearing headings rather than cute or cryptic ones.
- Spell out unfamiliar abbreviations on first use.
- Use numerals or spelled-out numbers consistently with the publication's
  needs; preserve numerals for dates, prices, measurements, and data when they
  improve scanning.
- Keep parentheticals and nested clauses under control.
- Use lists only when the material is genuinely list-shaped. A weekend read
  should mainly be prose.
- Avoid tables in the main text unless comparison would be materially harder in
  prose.

## Output variants

### Clean Markdown

Default to a title, optional subtitle or standfirst, descriptive headings,
normal paragraphs, sparing lists, and a restrained notes or sources section.

### Text-to-speech

Write for the ear:

- turn most lists and tables into flowing prose;
- expand abbreviations on first mention;
- weave numbers and short quotations into sentences;
- avoid footnote markers, symbols, stacked punctuation, and visual cross-
  references;
- use brief spoken transitions when the listener needs orientation.

### EPUB

Avoid inline Markdown footnotes when they will disrupt reading or narration.
Collect numbered notes at the end of each chapter or in a final notes section,
according to the user's preference. Keep note text useful and compact.

### Pure audio

Strip Markdown, convert headings into natural spoken transitions, replace
visual lists with spoken enumeration, and repeat essential terms deliberately
when it aids retention.

## External publication

Remove private references to the user, their company, and internal products
unless the piece is intentionally personal or branded. Include a byline only
when requested. Preserve the author's legitimate perspective without leaking
context that an outside reader cannot understand.
