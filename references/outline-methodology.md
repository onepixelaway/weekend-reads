# Outline Methodology

Use this process to create a weekend-length reading journey before drafting
prose. The outline is a design artifact and an approval gate, not a formality.

## 1. Write the reader promise

Complete this sentence in plain language:

> By the end, a curious reader who begins with [starting knowledge] will
> understand [subject] well enough to [new understanding, judgment, or action].

Then identify the governing question. A subject is not a question. “The history
of maps” is a subject; “how maps became instruments of power and everyday
navigation” gives the piece something to uncover.

Check that the promise can be delivered honestly in roughly 8,000–12,000 words.
If not, narrow the period, geography, audience, mechanism, or outcome.

## 2. Generate three structural options

Offer structures that alter the reader's experience, not just the headings.
Choose from these patterns or invent a better one for the material:

- **Journey:** Begin with the reader's current picture, complicate it, descend
  into mechanisms and evidence, then return with a changed view.
- **Layers:** Move from visible effects to the systems, incentives, history,
  and constraints beneath them.
- **Questions:** Organize around a sequence of increasingly difficult questions.
- **Landscape:** Map the major actors, approaches, schools, or regions, then
  explain the tensions connecting them.
- **Chronology with a purpose:** Use time to reveal cause and change, not to list
  events.
- **Case-led:** Let a small number of well-chosen cases expose the larger system.
- **Practical field guide:** Move from orientation to diagnosis, choices,
  practice, failure modes, and judgment.

For each option, provide:

- a two- or three-sentence description of the reading experience;
- the main advantage;
- the main risk;
- a provisional sequence of parts;
- the kinds of evidence or examples it needs.

Recommend one and explain why. A weighted blend is welcome when each component
has a clear job.

## 3. Build the arc

Most successful weekend guides contain five movements, even when their chapter
titles do not advertise them:

1. **Orientation:** Give the reader a foothold and establish why the question
   matters.
2. **Deepening:** Supply history, concepts, or mechanisms that change the
   initial picture.
3. **Complication:** Introduce tradeoffs, failures, disagreement, or evidence
   that resists the easy account.
4. **Consequence:** Show what the deeper picture changes in practice, judgment,
   or expectation.
5. **Landing:** Synthesize the argument and return to the reader promise.

Do not force every topic into this exact order. Use it to detect an outline that
only accumulates information and never develops.

## 4. Expand to chapter contracts

Aim for six to nine chapters or substantial sections. For a 10,000-word guide,
the body will often occupy 8,500–9,200 words, leaving room for an introduction,
ending, and notes.

Give every chapter:

- a content-bearing working title;
- a one-sentence contract stating what changes for the reader;
- two to five subordinate questions or beats;
- the evidence, cases, scenes, or reasoning it requires;
- the tension or uncertainty it must preserve;
- a rough word budget;
- the bridge it creates in the overall arc.

Avoid equal budgets by habit. A hinge chapter may deserve twice the space of an
orientation chapter.

## 5. Check pacing and texture

Mark the outline by function: explanation, history, case, mechanism, tension,
practice, synthesis. Long runs of the same function usually feel flat.

Ensure the plan includes:

- an early concrete foothold;
- regular movement between abstraction and observed detail;
- at least one serious counter-position or complicating case when the topic is
  contested;
- moments where the reader can consolidate what has changed;
- a final chapter that does new synthesizing work.

Do not prescribe a story, anecdote, or quotation before confirming that a real
source supports it.

## 6. Pressure-test

Review the outline from four angles:

- **Coverage:** What would a well-informed reader be surprised not to find?
- **Redundancy:** Which chapters make the same move with different nouns?
- **Sequence:** What knowledge does each chapter assume, and has the reader
  received it?
- **Momentum:** Where might curiosity stall? Is there an unresolved question or
  consequence carrying the reader forward?

Also test the title and reader promise against the actual outline. A rich
outline can still answer a different question from the one promised.

Apply the fixes rather than merely listing them. Briefly report the material
changes so the user can evaluate the reasoning.

## 7. Lock the outline

Save or present the approved outline as a standalone artifact. Record the
reader promise, grounding, chapter contracts, evidence needs, word budgets, and
open questions.

When the user asked for an outline, stop here. When the full guide is requested,
use this artifact as the drafting contract. Expand and trim within chapter
contracts; obtain approval before materially changing the structure.
